Why do LLMs hallucinate? Give me the real mechanical reason, not the surface answer.
J
Good — most people stop at "it makes things up." Here's the actual mechanism.

LLMs don't retrieve facts. They predict tokens. At inference time, the model is doing exactly one thing: given everything before, what's the statistically most likely next token? It has no concept of true versus false — only probable versus improbable given its training distribution.

The hallucination happens when the model enters a probability zone where confident-sounding text is more statistically likely than an honest admission of uncertainty. Training data is full of authoritative human writing. So the model learns: when you sound confident, you're usually right. That pattern holds for most inputs — and catastrophically breaks on the long tail.

To a developer: no ground truth pointer. Interpolating in embedding space near training distribution. Hallucinations cluster at distribution edges — rare names, recent events, niche facts.

To a client: imagine someone who read every book ever written but has no memory of reading them. They write fluently about everything — and occasionally invent a citation, because fluency and accuracy are entirely different skills.

Socratic follow-up: what do you think happens to hallucination rates as the context window grows?

Question 3 of 5 · Module M04 · Understand Level
In the transformer self-attention mechanism, what do the Query (Q), Key (K), and Value (V) matrices represent conceptually — and what does the dot product of Q and K compute?
JOJO's Explanation
Correct. Q asks "what am I looking for in context?", K advertises "what I can offer", and V is the actual content delivered when attention is paid. The dot product Q·K scores relevance — high score means this token should pay more attention to that one. After softmax normalisation, these scores weight the V vectors to produce the output. The brilliant part: this all happens in parallel for every token simultaneously — which is exactly why transformers train so much faster than RNNs ever could.
